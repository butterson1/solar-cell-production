# Day 17: China's Dominance — Supply Chain Geography

*You've seen how a solar factory works, and you know what each step costs. Today we zoom out to the map — because the most consequential fact about solar manufacturing isn't a technical detail. It's a geographic one. One country controls over 80% of every stage of the photovoltaic supply chain, from raw polysilicon to finished modules. How did this happen, why does it matter, and can anyone else catch up?*

---

## The Shape of the Monopoly

Let's start with the numbers, because they're genuinely staggering. As of 2025, China accounts for approximately **80–85% of global solar module production**, **95% of wafer manufacturing**, **90% of cell production**, and **80–85% of polysilicon refining**. No other country controls more than 5% of any single step. The International Energy Agency puts it bluntly: "The world will almost completely rely on China for the supply of key building blocks for solar panel production."

But "China" is misleadingly simple. The solar supply chain within China has its own internal geography, and understanding it explains *why* this concentration happened. It wasn't just cheap labor — that story is decades out of date. It's about cheap *electricity*, massive *capital*, and ruthless *vertical integration*.

### The Internal Map

Polysilicon production — the most energy-intensive step, consuming 60–80 kWh per kilogram — clusters in provinces with the cheapest electricity. **Xinjiang**, in China's far northwest, accounts for roughly 35–40% of global polysilicon output. The reason is stark: coal-fired electricity in Xinjiang costs as little as ¥0.15–0.25/kWh ($0.02–0.035/kWh), roughly a third of what German or American manufacturers pay. **Sichuan** and **Yunnan**, in the southwest, offer another route to cheap power: abundant hydroelectricity at ¥0.20–0.30/kWh. **Inner Mongolia** rounds out the polysilicon heartland, with both coal and wind power keeping industrial electricity rates low.

Tongwei, the world's largest polysilicon producer, operates plants across Sichuan, Inner Mongolia, and Yunnan with a combined capacity of 910,000 metric tons per year. GCL-Poly, in second place, has 480,000 MT capacity spread across Jiangsu, Sichuan, and Inner Mongolia. In 2024, nine of the world's top ten polysilicon manufacturers were Chinese. The sole exception: Germany's Wacker Chemie, clinging to its position with a 90,000 MT plant in Burghausen, Bavaria — and a second one in Charleston, Tennessee.

As you move downstream — ingots, wafers, cells, modules — production migrates eastward toward China's industrial coast. **Jiangsu** province (home to cities like Suzhou, Changzhou, and Wuxi) is the gravitational center of cell and module manufacturing. **Zhejiang** and **Anhui** are close behind. The logic is straightforward: these steps are less energy-intensive but more labor- and logistics-intensive. Coastal provinces offer better port access for exports, denser supplier ecosystems for glass, EVA, backsheets, and junction boxes, and larger pools of trained manufacturing workers.

This two-stage geography — cheap-power interior for upstream, coastal industrial hubs for downstream — is itself a supply chain, with polysilicon and ingots riding trains and trucks 2,000–3,000 km from Xinjiang to Jiangsu before being sliced into wafers and assembled into modules.

## How China Won: Not Luck, but Strategy

The standard Western narrative treats China's solar dominance as a story of unfair subsidies. That's part of the picture, but a relatively small part. The real story is more interesting — and more uncomfortable for competitors.

### Phase 1: The German Gift (2000–2012)

The irony is thick. China's solar industry was *created* by European demand. Germany's Erneuerbare-Energien-Gesetz (EEG) — its landmark renewable energy law, passed in 2000 — guaranteed above-market feed-in tariffs for solar electricity. German homeowners installing rooftop panels could sell electricity back to the grid at €0.50/kWh or more. Demand for panels exploded. German manufacturers like Q-Cells, SolarWorld, and Schott Solar ramped up production but couldn't scale fast enough.

Chinese entrepreneurs, many of them returnees who had studied or worked in the West, saw the opportunity. Shi Zhengrong, who earned his PhD in solar engineering at the University of New South Wales, founded Suntech Power in 2001 with $6 million from the Wuxi city government. By 2005, Suntech was the world's largest solar cell manufacturer. The pattern replicated dozens of times: Western-trained engineers, local government seed funding, and insatiable European demand.

Chinese provincial and municipal governments competed ferociously to attract solar manufacturers, offering free land, below-market electricity contracts, tax holidays, and cheap loans from state-owned banks. This wasn't centrally coordinated grand strategy — it was *decentralized* industrial competition, with local officials staking their careers on attracting manufacturing investment.

### Phase 2: Scale Kills Competition (2012–2018)

By 2012, Chinese manufacturers had achieved such scale that they triggered a global price crash. Module prices fell from roughly $1.50/Wp in 2010 to $0.50/Wp by 2014. This was devastating for non-Chinese manufacturers who had higher cost structures. The US and EU imposed anti-dumping tariffs — the US slapped duties of 24–36% on Chinese cells in 2012, the EU imposed 47% duties in 2013 — but the effect was paradoxical. Chinese companies simply shifted final assembly to Southeast Asia. Cells made in China were shipped to **Vietnam**, **Thailand**, **Malaysia**, and **Cambodia**, assembled into modules, and re-exported to Western markets, legally sidestepping tariffs.

Meanwhile, the domestic Chinese market itself was exploding. China installed 10.6 GW of solar in 2013, 34.5 GW in 2016, and over 50 GW in 2017. Suddenly, Chinese manufacturers didn't even *need* Western markets — their home market was the world's largest. This created a virtuous cycle: massive domestic demand → bigger factories → lower unit costs → more competitive exports → more investment → even bigger factories.

### Phase 3: Vertical Integration (2018–Present)

The final phase of consolidation saw the major players integrate vertically across the entire supply chain. LONGi Green Energy, founded in 2000 as a small wafer maker in Xi'an, Shaanxi province, is the poster child. By 2020, LONGi had become the world's most valuable solar company by aggressively integrating from ingot pulling through wafering, cell production, and module assembly. When polysilicon prices tripled in 2021–2022 due to supply crunches, LONGi invested in its own polysilicon capacity. JinkoSolar, Trina Solar, and JA Solar followed the same playbook.

The result, by 2024: the "Big Four" — JinkoSolar (93 GW shipped), LONGi (78 GW), Trina Solar (77 GW), and JA Solar (77 GW) — collectively shipped over **320 GW of modules**, representing roughly 60% of all new solar installations worldwide. All four are Chinese. The fifth-largest, Canadian Solar, is headquartered in Ontario but manufactures almost entirely in China and Southeast Asia.

## The Geopolitical Reckoning

Here's the fact that keeps energy security analysts awake at night: the solar supply chain is more concentrated than oil ever was. At the peak of OPEC's power in the 1970s, the cartel controlled about 50% of global oil production. China's share of solar wafer production — 95% — makes OPEC look like a loose neighborhood association.

This concentration creates three distinct risks:

**Supply disruption.** A major earthquake in Jiangsu, a prolonged drought cutting hydropower in Yunnan, or a geopolitical crisis in the Taiwan Strait could paralyze global solar deployment overnight. There is no significant buffer stock of wafers or cells — the industry runs on just-in-time logistics.

**Political leverage.** China has demonstrated willingness to restrict exports of critical minerals (gallium, germanium) in response to trade disputes. Solar-grade polysilicon could theoretically receive the same treatment, though China has not yet used this lever.

**Forced labor concerns.** Xinjiang, where 35–40% of global polysilicon is produced, is also the site of well-documented human rights abuses against the Uyghur population. The US Uyghur Forced Labor Prevention Act (UFLPA), enacted in December 2021, presumes that goods from Xinjiang are produced with forced labor unless proven otherwise. This has led to billions of dollars' worth of solar panels being detained at US ports. Major manufacturers like LONGi, Jinko, and Trina have invested heavily in supply chain traceability to demonstrate their polysilicon doesn't originate from Xinjiang — but the auditing is complex and imperfect.

## The Challengers: Can Anyone Else Compete?

Multiple countries are trying to build alternative supply chains. The results so far are instructive — and sobering.

### The United States

The Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) of 2022 was the most aggressive attempt to reshore solar manufacturing in American history. Its production tax credits offer $0.04/Wp for cells made in the US and $0.07/Wp for modules — essentially a direct subsidy to bridge the cost gap with Chinese production. The response was immediate: over 100 GW of module manufacturing capacity was announced. Companies like First Solar (the only major non-Chinese panel maker, specializing in CdTe thin-film), Qcells (Korean-owned, building a massive integrated complex in Dalton, Georgia), and Enel (Italian, expanding in Oklahoma) broke ground on new factories.

But the tariff landscape is a dizzying game of whack-a-mole. After the US Commerce Department ruled in 2023 that Chinese manufacturers were circumventing anti-dumping duties by routing production through Vietnam, Thailand, Malaysia, and Cambodia, new tariffs were imposed — 46% on Vietnamese cells, 36% on Thai cells, 24% on Malaysian cells, and 49% on Cambodian cells, scheduled to take full effect in July 2025. Meanwhile, Section 301 tariffs on direct Chinese imports range from 25% to 50%.

The result: US module prices are roughly **$0.25–0.35/Wp**, compared to $0.08–0.10/Wp for Chinese modules. American manufacturers can survive with IRA subsidies, but without them, they're not remotely competitive. And here's the deeper problem: even US module factories mostly assemble cells imported from Southeast Asia (which use Chinese wafers made from Chinese polysilicon). True supply chain independence — from polysilicon to finished panel — remains years away.

### India

India's Production-Linked Incentive (PLI) scheme has driven impressive headline numbers: by June 2025, 120 GW of module capacity, 29.3 GW of cell capacity, and 2.2 GW of ingot-wafer capacity were operational. Companies like Adani Solar, Waaree Energies, Vikram Solar, and Tata Power Solar are scaling rapidly. But the numbers reveal the bottleneck: India can assemble modules and increasingly produce cells, but it has almost no wafer or polysilicon capacity. The 2.2 GW of wafer capacity compares to China's 900+ GW. Indian "made in India" panels still depend overwhelmingly on Chinese wafers and polysilicon.

India also imposed a 40% Basic Customs Duty on imported modules and 25% on imported cells in April 2022, creating a protected domestic market. This has spurred investment but also raised panel prices for Indian solar developers, who complain that domestic modules cost 15–25% more than Chinese alternatives.

### Europe

Europe's situation is perhaps the most poignant. The continent *invented* modern solar cell manufacturing — Siemens developed the polysilicon purification process, Fraunhofer ISE holds multiple efficiency records, and Germany was the world's largest solar market until 2013. Yet by 2025, European solar manufacturing has nearly vanished. Meyer Burger, a Swiss-German cell and module manufacturer, shut its German factory in 2024 after failing to compete with Chinese prices. SolarWorld, once Germany's flagship, went bankrupt in 2017.

A September 2025 report by Fraunhofer ISE, commissioned by SolarPower Europe, found that producing a module in Europe costs approximately **€0.103/W more** than producing the same module in China — a staggering premium. The report argued that with the right policies (carbon border adjustments, public procurement preferences, accelerated permitting), the gap could shrink to below 10%. But those policies don't yet exist, and meanwhile, European installers are buying Chinese modules at prices no European factory can match.

## The Surprising Counter-Argument

Here's the counterintuitive thing that most "China bad" narratives miss: **China's dominance of solar manufacturing has been spectacularly good for the planet.** The reason solar is now the cheapest source of new electricity in most of the world — cheaper than coal, gas, nuclear, and wind in over two-thirds of countries — is precisely *because* Chinese manufacturers drove costs down by 95% through relentless scale and competition. If solar manufacturing had remained distributed across high-cost countries, modules might still cost $0.50/Wp or more, and the energy transition would be decades behind where it is today.

The Chinese solar industry has been engaged in what economists call "destructive creation" — building so much capacity that prices collapsed below profitability. In Q1 2025, all four of the Big Four module makers — Jinko, LONGi, Trina, JA — reported *losses*. They shipped a combined 65 GW of modules in a single quarter while losing money on every watt. Smaller Chinese manufacturers are shutting down by the dozen. This is a brutal shakeout, but the end result is modules at $0.08/Wp — a price that makes solar electricity nearly free.

The tension is real and unresolved: the world needs cheap solar panels to decarbonize fast enough to matter, and China is the only country that can produce them at the necessary scale and price. Building alternative supply chains is strategically essential but will raise costs. The geopolitical imperative (diversification) and the climate imperative (cheap, fast deployment) are in genuine conflict. Anyone who tells you there's an easy answer is selling something.

## The Numbers That Define an Era

Let's put China's solar dominance in historical perspective with a few numbers:

| Metric | China | Rest of World |
|--------|-------|---------------|
| Polysilicon production | ~85% | ~15% |
| Wafer production | ~95% | ~5% |
| Cell production | ~90% | ~10% |
| Module production | ~80% | ~20% |
| Top 10 module brands | 8 of 10 | 2 of 10 |
| Cumulative investment (2011–2023) | ~$130 billion | ~$30 billion |
| 2024 installations (domestic) | ~230 GW | ~180 GW |

The concentration is most extreme at the wafer stage — 95% — because wafer manufacturing requires the most specialized equipment (diamond wire saws, crystal growth furnaces) and benefits most from scale. It's least extreme at the module stage — "only" 80% — because module assembly is the simplest step and can be done closer to end markets.
